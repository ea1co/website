Our approach

Over more than a decade of studying how communities actually form — how people gather, participate, and influence each other — we developed a set of principles. Your audience has an audience. Enable fans to be better fans. Audiences are networks of participation.

These ideas shape every piece of work we put out. So we started building them directly into the instruments we use every day. The instruments hold the underlying structure — the rhythm section — so the team can focus on the higher-level thinking: interpretation, judgment, improvisation.

Each tool here exists because the work required it. Listening tuned to how communities actually behave. Analysis that preserves the diversity of motivations within an audience. Systems that pressure-test whether our thinking aligns with our principles.

We build them iteratively, in conversation with AI, shaped by editorial judgment. The team leaves feedback, we think it through together, and changes ship the same day. The tools stay close to the work because they grow directly out of it.

Team Radar

The team shares stories in Slack all day — articles, references, things that caught their attention. This tool surfaces what the team is collectively paying attention to and gives it a stage. It's the same move we pioneered with fans: find the signal in what people are already talking about, elevate it, give credit, create new connections. What happens when we turn that practice inward?

Trend Distance

How far does a trend travel from where it starts? This tool measures the distance between a signal first appearing on social platforms and how quickly it shows up across news media — a way of seeing how ideas move from communities into the broader conversation, and how fast that cascade happens.

What's next

We think a new kind of agency is emerging.

An agency has always been a collection of people who think well together. The question is where that intelligence lives. In the senior team's intuition? In the methodology deck? In the tools everyone actually uses?

We're exploring what happens when you write your own software — designed specifically for how this team sees. When the way the team sees — how they listen, how they read a community, how they judge what matters — is carried by the systems they work through every day. The thinking doesn't disappear into a framework. It stays active in the work.

The tools above are just what we're building right now. There are more ideas behind them. Each one is a small experiment in the same larger question: what does it look like when an agency's cognition isn't locked in people's heads, but distributed across the practice itself?
